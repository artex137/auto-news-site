Three Low-Cost Ways to Ease South Asia’s Water Shortage: A Shocking Reality

The dried bed of the ancient Siranda Lake in Pakistan stands as a haunting testament to the water shortage crisis gripping South Asia. As reported by the World Economic Forum, the situation is dire, with countries like Pakistan—ranked as the eighth most vulnerable nation to climate shocks—facing catastrophic consequences. Families in rural Balochistan are forced to trek miles daily just to secure drinking water, a grim reality that underscores the severity of the climate crisis.

In May 2025, a staggering 47 volunteers from the Balochistan Youth Action Committee (BYAC) gathered in Quetta for a two-day summit. Their mission? To confront the pressing climate challenges that plague their region. No surprise here—the overwhelming consensus was that water shortages and droughts are at the top of their list of concerns. In fact, a shocking 70% of Pakistan's domestic water needs are met by groundwater, and yet, despite the monsoon rains that flood the region, this essential resource remains critically scarce.

“Life is, if water is,” said a 20-year-old BYAC volunteer, reflecting the harsh reality faced by many.

As the crisis deepens, the consequences become increasingly grim. Migration, loss of vegetation, and destruction of livelihoods are just a few of the devastating impacts that arise from the lack of water. However, amidst this chaos, the World Economic Forum has proposed three low-cost, scalable solutions that could help South Asian countries like Pakistan increase their natural water tables. But can these solutions truly address the monumental challenges ahead?

Water Security: A Global Issue

Water security is not just a local issue; it's a global crisis. Currently, a staggering 80% of wastewater flows untreated back into the environment, while 780 million people worldwide lack access to improved water sources. Alarmingly, by 2030, we may face a daunting 40% global gap between water supply and demand. The World Economic Forum’s Water Possible Platform is supposedly on a mission to tackle this pressing issue, but are their efforts enough?

They tout partnerships with groups like the 2030 Water Resources Group, which claims to have facilitated a whopping $1 billion in investments aimed at closing the gap between global water demand and supply. But with the clock ticking, can these partnerships deliver real results before it’s too late?

Three Proposed Solutions: Hope or Hype?

So, what are these miraculous solutions that the World Economic Forum is championing? First up: percolation pits. These small dug-out areas, filled with stones or gravel, are designed to let rainwater soak into the ground. Known as 'Zai pits' in the Sahel region of Africa, this traditional water harvesting technique has been around for ages. During the dry season, farmers dig small basins to improve water retention, capturing rainwater to replenish the natural water table. Sounds simple, right? But will it really work on the scale needed to combat the water crisis in South Asia?

Next, we have groundwater recharge—a natural process where water from rainfall infiltrates the ground, replenishing aquifers and sustaining long-term water availability. The Government of Pakistan has already piloted 50 ‘recharge wells’, managing to conserve over 10 million gallons of water in less than a year. This approach is touted as low-cost and effective, but can it be implemented widely enough to make a difference?

Finally, there’s the call to plant native trees. This strategy aims to improve the natural water table by enhancing soil permeability and promoting rainwater infiltration while reducing surface runoff. In India’s Aravalli Hills, reforestation efforts with native species like Dhok (Anogeissus Pendula) have reportedly improved groundwater levels significantly. Yet, can these initiatives be scaled up to truly impact the broader region?

Urgency for Action

All three approaches share a common theme: they are low-cost, community-driven, and rapidly deployable. But while the World Economic Forum’s suggestions sound promising, the urgency of the situation cannot be overstated. South Asian governments, NGOs, and private partners need to mobilize quickly and decisively to implement these models before the next wave of climate-driven displacement hits their shores. The time for talking is over; action is needed now!

In a world where climate change is an ever-looming threat, the future of millions hangs in the balance. Will the proposed solutions be the lifeline South Asia desperately needs, or are they merely a drop in the bucket against an ocean of challenges? Only time will tell, but one thing is certain: the clock is ticking, and the stakes have never been higher.
