Trump's war against wind extends into electric transmission projects - Fortune

Published 2025-08-14 14:54 UTC

Trump's War on Wind: The Battle for America's Energy Future

In a striking display of political theater, former President Donald Trump has unleashed a ferocious assault on renewable energy, particularly targeting the ambitious Grain Belt Express project, a behemoth electric transmission initiative that promises to reshape the energy landscape of the United States. This $11 billion, 800-mile endeavor, designed to transport clean energy from Kansas to Indiana, has become the epicenter of a political storm, as the Trump administration pulls the rug out from under what could be the largest electric transmission project in U.S. history.

According to Jordan Blum, Energy Editor at Fortune, the Grain Belt Express is poised to deliver enough power to energize approximately 4 million homes or 50 data centers. Yet, despite the project's potential to support the nation's growing electricity needs, the Trump administration has thrown it into disarray. In late July, the administration abruptly rescinded a crucial $4.9 billion federal loan guarantee, a move that has left the project's future hanging by a thread.

“Windmills should not be allowed,” Trump proclaimed in July, echoing his long-standing disdain for wind energy. “It is the worst form of energy, the most expensive form of energy.”

The official narrative from the White House cites concerns over the alleged hurried approval process under the Biden administration and landowners' grievances regarding forced land acquisitions through eminent domain. However, critics argue that this is merely a façade for Trump's personal vendetta against wind power. Political opponents have gone so far as to label the Grain Belt a “massive green energy scam,” showcasing the shifting battleground from localized NIMBYism to the very corridors of power in Washington, D.C.

As the battle rages on, American Clean Power Association CEO Jason Grumet has voiced his concerns, stating, “The administration is seeking to undermine both the generation of clean power and the infrastructure that supports it.” He lamented the increasing polarization surrounding the issue, emphasizing that “you can’t give electrons a political loyalty test to decide whether or not they belong on the nation’s electric grid.” The implications of this political maneuvering are staggering, as they threaten to derail not just the Grain Belt but potentially other critical transmission projects across the country.

The Department of Energy (DOE) has defended its position, asserting that the loan guarantee was unnecessary and could pose a taxpayer liability. A spokesperson for the DOE stated, “The Department of Energy will continue to use all tools at its disposal... to support projects that advance baseload energy sources, strengthen the grid and lower costs for the American people.” Notably, the administration's focus on “baseload” energy sources—namely coal, natural gas, and nuclear—underscores its clear aversion to wind and solar power, which are deemed intermittent at best.

Despite the setbacks, Invenergy, the developer behind the Grain Belt Express, remains steadfast. The company insists that the project will proceed, arguing that it will also facilitate power generation from coal and gas plants. “America is energy dominant and an AI powerhouse,” they declared, asserting that the Grain Belt Express will be “America’s largest power pipeline.” They maintain that even without the loan guarantee, the project will yield billions in energy cost savings, bolster grid reliability, and create thousands of jobs.

The saga of the Grain Belt Express is intricately tied to a broader political landscape, with Republican figures like U.S. Senator Josh Hawley and Missouri's Attorney General Andrew Bailey leading the charge against the project. Hawley has been particularly vocal, labeling it a “reckless green energy scam” and orchestrating meetings with Energy Secretary Chris Wright, reportedly with Trump's blessing, to ensure the cancellation of the loan guarantee. The political machinations behind this decision are troubling, as they raise questions about the future of renewable energy in America.

“If projects can’t count on certainty even after being approved and reviewed upon appeal, America can’t count on ever getting steel in the ground,” Invenergy lamented in a statement, highlighting the precarious nature of the current political environment.

As the controversy unfolds, the construction contractor for Grain Belt, Quanta Services, has opted to sideline the project in its official backlog, citing the uncertainty surrounding its future. Quanta CEO Earl “Duke” Austin expressed cautious optimism, stating, “We’ll work together to try to get it across the finish line, and we really like the project. I do believe at some point it will get built.”

Meanwhile, the central U.S. wind belt, which boasts nearly 1,000 gigawatts of wind energy capacity, remains largely untapped due to inadequate infrastructure. Kansas, for instance, has only about 10 gigawatts of wind power operational, largely because of the lack of transmission lines to deliver this energy to densely populated areas. The pressing question now is whether the Trump administration will extend its regulatory onslaught to other vital electricity transmission projects.

With the largest onshore transmission initiative currently under construction being Pattern Energy’s SunZia Transmission project in Arizona and New Mexico, analysts predict it is unlikely to face similar scrutiny at this late stage. However, the future of transmission construction remains precarious. As Brett Castelli, an energy and utility analyst for Morningstar, noted, while transmission construction capex is at an all-time high for utilities, spending on new lines is stagnating due to a focus on modernizing existing infrastructure.

The stakes have never been higher. As the demand for electricity surges—driven in part by the burgeoning artificial intelligence sector—America's energy future hangs in the balance, caught in a whirlwind of political maneuvering and regulatory uncertainty. The Grain Belt Express may just be the litmus test for the nation's commitment to embracing renewable energy or retreating into the shadows of fossil fuels.
