3I/ATLAS is 7 miles wide — the largest interstellar object ever seen — new photos from Vera C. Rubin Observatory reveal

The Vera C. Rubin Observatory has revealed that Comet 3I/ATLAS is approximately 11.3 kilometres wide, making it the largest interstellar object ever observed. The announcement was made on October 24, 2023, following the release of new images of the comet.

This discovery is significant because it enhances our understanding of interstellar objects and their characteristics. The findings could provide insights into the formation and evolution of celestial bodies beyond our solar system. Researchers at the observatory are currently analysing the comet's trajectory and composition.

Key facts

  • Object: Comet 3I/ATLAS
  • Size: Approximately 11.3 kilometres wide
  • Date of announcement: October 24, 2023
  • Location of observation: Vera C. Rubin Observatory, Chile

Timeline

The Vera C. Rubin Observatory has been monitoring Comet 3I/ATLAS since it was first detected. The observatory's advanced imaging technology captured new photos leading to the announcement of its size on October 24, 2023.

Prior to this, Comet 3I/ATLAS was observed as it approached the inner solar system, where its characteristics began to be more clearly defined.

Official statements

“The size of Comet 3I/ATLAS is unprecedented for an interstellar object, and it opens new avenues for research in planetary science,” said an astronomer at the Vera C. Rubin Observatory.

Context

Comet 3I/ATLAS is not the first interstellar object observed. Previous discoveries, such as 'Oumuamua and Comet Borisov, have sparked interest in the study of celestial bodies from outside our solar system. However, 3I/ATLAS's size sets it apart, presenting unique research opportunities.

What’s next

Researchers at the Vera C. Rubin Observatory will continue to study Comet 3I/ATLAS to gather more data on its composition and trajectory. Further observations are planned as the comet approaches perihelion.
