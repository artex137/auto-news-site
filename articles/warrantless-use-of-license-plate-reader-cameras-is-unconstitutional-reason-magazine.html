Warrantless Use of License Plate Reader Cameras Is Unconstitutional

In an era where privacy seems to be a quaint relic of the past, we are confronted with the alarming reality of widespread surveillance. The latest revelation comes from the Institute for Justice (I.J.), a public-interest law firm, which has launched the Plate Privacy Project. This initiative aims to combat the arbitrary and unrestrained use of Automatic License Plate Reader (ALPR) cameras that have infiltrated thousands of American communities. Yes, you heard it right—our very own government is tracking our every move with technology that sounds like it belongs in a dystopian novel!

ALPRs are not just sci-fi fantasies; they are here, and they are everywhere. According to the Electronic Frontier Foundation (EFF), these systems utilize high-speed cameras combined with optical character recognition technology to identify license plates and convert them into machine-readable text. They’re mounted on poles at traffic stops or on police cars, capturing every vehicle that drives by at a staggering rate of up to 2,000 plates per minute. Imagine the sheer volume of data being collected! The I.J. notes that all this captured information is uploaded to a central database, complete with time, date, and GPS coordinates. What could possibly go wrong?

“Once that happens, officials can search the database for any vehicle they wish, all without a warrant.”

Indeed, the implications are chilling. Unlike red-light or speed cameras that operate on specific violations, these cameras indiscriminately photograph every vehicle, creating profiles with identifying information stored in a massive database. The ability for law enforcement agencies across the country to share this data means that your movements are being tracked without your consent. It's a surveillance state nightmare that threatens our privacy, security, and freedom of movement!

The Plate Privacy Project aims to fight back against this invasive surveillance by proposing model legislation in state legislatures, partnering with local activists, and strengthening Fourth Amendment protections against warrantless surveillance. The project's website even promises to provide reliable data about the extent of ALPR surveillance in local communities. But will this be enough to turn the tide?

Recent statistics raise eyebrows about the prevalence of ALPR technology among law enforcement. A 2024 Congressional Research Service report revealed that nearly 90% of sheriffs' offices with 500 or more sworn deputies reported using ALPR technology. In fact, 100% of police departments serving over 1 million residents have adopted these invasive systems. This is not just a minor issue; it is a widespread violation of our civil liberties!

Consider the harrowing case of Brittney Gilliam, who was pulled over in August 2020 by Colorado police officers after an ALPR flagged her vehicle as stolen. The officers went so far as to handcuff her daughter and nieces, forcing them to lie face down on the pavement. It was only after the officers realized that Gilliam's SUV had Colorado plates—while the stolen vehicle was a motorcycle with a Montana tag—that they released her. This is not just a simple mistake; it is a glaring example of the consequences of unrestrained surveillance!

But the story doesn't end there. In Sacramento, California, officials recently discovered that the sheriff's office was sharing license plate information with out-of-state agencies, despite being prohibited by state law. Similarly, in Illinois, a state law bars the sharing of ALPR data with immigration enforcement authorities. Yet, records suggest that U.S. Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE) may have accessed ALPR cameras in over 5,000 communities nationwide. What happened to our rights?

“Flock is building a giant camera network that records people's comings and goings across the nation.”

The private company Flock Safety operates ALPR systems in Illinois and has over 40,000 cameras nationwide. The American Civil Liberties Union has raised alarms about Flock's network, claiming it contributes to a centralized mass surveillance system of Orwellian proportions. One of Flock's clients, the city of Norfolk, Virginia, installed 172 ALPRs in 2023, leading to a federal lawsuit by I.J. The lawsuit argues that using or accessing this ALPR system constitutes an unlawful search, violating the Fourth Amendment. How many more cities are watching us with these unblinking eyes?

In Norfolk, the situation is dire. The city plans to acquire an additional 65 cameras, expanding its surveillance capabilities even further. Thankfully, there is some hope; in February, U.S. District Court Chief Judge Mark S. Davis allowed I.J.'s lawsuit to proceed, denying the city's motion to dismiss. This could be a pivotal moment in the fight against this invasive technology.

As the Plate Privacy Project gains momentum, it appears that the American public is beginning to recognize the dangers of unchecked surveillance. Communities across the nation have recently terminated ALPR contracts with Flock Safety due to public backlash. Perhaps, just perhaps, we are waking up to the reality that our privacy is worth fighting for.

As we march into a future increasingly dominated by surveillance technology, the question remains: Are we willing to sacrifice our freedoms for the illusion of security? The answer must be a resounding no!
