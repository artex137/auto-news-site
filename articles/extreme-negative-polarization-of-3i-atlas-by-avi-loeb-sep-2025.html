Extreme Negative Polarization of Comet 3I/ATLAS Identified

A new study has identified comet 3I/ATLAS as having unique polarization properties, distinguishing it from solar system bodies and the interstellar comet 2I/Borisov. The findings were reported in September 2025, highlighting the comet's extreme negative polarization and its implications for understanding interstellar objects.

This discovery is significant as it reveals the comet’s unprecedented polarimetric behavior, raising questions about its origin and composition. Researchers, including Avi Loeb, are preparing for further observations to gather more data on 3I/ATLAS's characteristics.

Key facts

  • Comet 3I/ATLAS exhibits an extreme negative polarization of −2.77 percent.
  • The phase angle at which this polarization is observed is 6.41 degrees.
  • The comet has a low inversion angle of 17.05 degrees.
  • 3I/ATLAS has an estimated nucleus diameter of up to 46 kilometers.
  • The comet will be imaged by the HiRISE camera on the Mars Reconnaissance Orbiter on October 3, 2025.
  • The gas plume around 3I/ATLAS is primarily composed of carbon dioxide (87% by mass).

Timeline

In September 2025, a study reported the distinct polarization properties of 3I/ATLAS, noting its negative polarization and low inversion angle. The comet's behavior deviates from known comets, indicating a potential new category of interstellar objects.

On October 3, 2025, the HiRISE camera on the Mars Reconnaissance Orbiter will capture images of 3I/ATLAS, promising to provide critical data on the nucleus's size.

Official statements

Avi Loeb stated, “Given these unprecedented anomalies, we must stay open-minded regarding the nature of 3I/ATLAS until we get better data.”

Context

The study of 3I/ATLAS follows the discoveries of previous interstellar objects, including 1I/`Oumuamua, which showed no signs of gas or dust, and 2I/Borisov, which behaved like a typical comet. The anomalies presented by 3I/ATLAS suggest it may represent a previously unobserved population of interstellar objects.

What’s next

Researchers will analyze the images captured by the HiRISE camera to confirm the size of 3I/ATLAS's nucleus. Further studies will continue to explore the comet's unique characteristics and potential origins.
