Eduardo Bolsonaro Praises President Trump's Tariffs on Brazil: A Machiavellian Turn of Events?

In a political drama that could rival the most outrageous soap operas, Eduardo Bolsonaro, son of former Brazilian President Jair Bolsonaro, has publicly lauded US President Donald Trump's hefty tariffs on Brazil. This bizarre twist comes amid a months-long lobbying campaign where Eduardo has attempted to sway the Trump administration into punishing Brazilian authorities for daring to put his father on trial for alleged coup charges. Is this a case of political genius or sheer desperation? The answers may shock you.

During an interview with the BBC in Washington, Eduardo Bolsonaro, an elected Congressman in Brazil, warned that more sanctions could be looming on the horizon. “There’s a very significant possibility regarding the application of sanctions and the extension of Magnitsky sanctions to other people,” he stated ominously. “You have on Secretary Marco Rubio’s desk, for example, the possibility of withdrawing visas, among other pressure mechanisms, to try to get Brazil out of this institutional crisis we’re experiencing.” It sounds like something straight out of a political thriller, but this is real life, folks.

At the White House, President Trump, in a fit of rage against Brazil, doubled down on his accusations of the country being a terrible trade partner. He absurdly claimed that the US runs a trade deficit with Brazil, when in fact, the US enjoyed a whopping $7.4 billion trade surplus with Brazil last year. In a moment of bizarre solidarity, Trump defended Jair Bolsonaro, calling the case against him a “political execution” and a “witch hunt,” reminiscent of his own legal troubles following the 2020 election. This has escalated tensions between the two largest democracies in the Americas to unprecedented heights.

Let’s pause for a moment to digest the implications of Trump’s tariffs, which he announced in July, raising them to a staggering 50% on Brazilian imports. This drastic measure was justified by Trump as a retaliation for Brazil's treatment of Jair Bolsonaro, the elder Bolsonaro now facing serious legal consequences for his actions. In a stunning show of diplomatic prowess, the US State Department also banned eight Brazilian Supreme Court justices from traveling to the US, including Alexandre de Moraes, the judge overseeing Jair Bolsonaro’s trial. Brazil's current President, Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva, denounced this as unacceptable interference in his country's justice system. But who cares about diplomacy when you can have drama?

In an unexpected twist, Eduardo Bolsonaro, who has been living in the US since March, claiming he is in “exile” out of fear of arrest upon returning to Brazil, defended his actions. “I believe freedom comes first, before the economy,” he asserted, as if the economic ramifications of his lobbying efforts didn’t matter. When confronted with the reality that a poll by the Quaest Institute showed that 79% of Brazilians believe the tariffs will negatively impact their lives, Eduardo had an answer: “Four in every 10 Brazilians were in support of the tariffs.” Talk about selective data interpretation!

What about the potential fallout for his family and the political landscape in Brazil? Eduardo claimed he is focused on “the next generation,” but acknowledged the concerns of his compatriots. “I’m not thinking about the next election,” he said, a statement that seems a tad disingenuous given his family's political ambitions. His father, despite being barred from running for public office until 2030 due to the coup charges, may still have aspirations to reclaim the presidency. Eduardo's ire is particularly directed at Alexandre de Moraes, the judge who imposed the ban on his father, and who is currently overseeing the coup trial.

In an ironic twist, Eduardo Bolsonaro, who has labeled Brazil as a “dictatorship,” was one of the congressmen who most vocally supported the 2019 proposal to reinstate the Institutional Act Number 5 (AI-5), a decree utilized during Brazil's military dictatorship to suppress dissent. So, can we really trust his claims of fighting for freedom? His rhetoric raises eyebrows, especially when he compares himself to women in Iran facing persecution for speaking out against their government. “What is going to happen with me if I go back to my country because I’m denouncing these dictators? I’m going to jail,” he lamented, as if he were the only one facing political peril.

As the drama unfolds, the question remains: will Eduardo’s machinations backfire, boosting support for Lula and further isolating the Bolsonaros? Or will he succeed in his quest to rally support against perceived injustices in Brazil? One thing is for sure—this saga is far from over, and the world will be watching as Brazil's political landscape continues to shift like quicksand.
