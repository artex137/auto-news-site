Does 3I/ATLAS Generate Its Own Light?

Avi Loeb, an astrophysicist at Harvard University, has raised questions about the nature of the interstellar object 3I/ATLAS, which was observed by the Hubble Space Telescope on July 21, 2025. The object appears to emit a glow likely from a coma, but lacks evidence of a cometary tail. This discovery suggests that 3I/ATLAS may generate its own light, rather than merely reflecting sunlight.

This finding has significant implications for our understanding of interstellar objects. Loeb and his colleague, Eric Keto, suggest that if 3I/ATLAS produces its own light, it could be much smaller than previously estimated. Current models suggest a diameter of up to 20 kilometers, which Loeb argues is untenable given the limited material available in interstellar space.

Key facts

  • Object: 3I/ATLAS
  • Observation Date: July 21, 2025
  • Observation Method: Hubble Space Telescope
  • Proximity to Mars: 28.96 million kilometers on October 3, 2025
  • Estimated Luminosity: 10 gigawatts
  • Diameter Estimate: Potentially less than 100 meters
  • Predicted Size from Reflection Model: Up to 20 kilometers

Timeline

On July 21, 2025, the Hubble Space Telescope captured the first significant image of 3I/ATLAS, revealing a glow ahead of its motion towards the Sun. Following this, Loeb and Keto analyzed the brightness profile of the object and proposed that the observed glow could indicate a central light source rather than sunlight reflection.

On the night of the discovery, Loeb participated in an annual soccer match at Harvard, where he scored two goals for the faculty team, but the students won 3-2. The following morning, he began calculations regarding the luminosity and size of 3I/ATLAS, leading to his current hypotheses about its nature.

Official statements

“The simplest interpretation is that the nucleus of 3I/ATLAS produces most of the light,” said Avi Loeb. “If 3I/ATLAS generates its own light, then it could be much smaller than expected.”

Context

3I/ATLAS is part of a growing interest in interstellar objects, following the discovery of 1I/`Oumuamua and 2I/Borisov. These previous objects have raised questions about the composition and behavior of materials originating from outside our solar system. The study of 3I/ATLAS could provide further insights into these phenomena.

What’s next

The HiRISE team on the Mars Reconnaissance Orbiter has expressed willingness to observe 3I/ATLAS during its close pass to Mars in October 2025. More data collected from these observations will aid in understanding the object's nature and characteristics.
