Does 3I/ATLAS Generate Its Own Light?

The Hubble Space Telescope has captured the best image of the interstellar object 3I/ATLAS on July 21, 2025, revealing a glow of light likely produced by a coma ahead of its motion toward the Sun. No evidence of a cometary tail has been found, and the glow is theorized to result from dust evaporation on the Sun-facing side of 3I/ATLAS.

This discovery raises questions about the nature of 3I/ATLAS and its size. Researchers, including Avi Loeb and Eric Keto, propose that the observed brightness profile may indicate that 3I/ATLAS generates its own light, which could mean it is significantly smaller than previously estimated. Current models suggest it could have a diameter of up to 20 kilometers, but this is seen as untenable due to the limited availability of rocky material in interstellar space.

Key facts

  • Date of image capture: July 21, 2025
  • Distance from Mars: 28.96 (+/-0.06) million kilometers, expected on October 3, 2025
  • Mass loss rate: 6–60 kilograms per second
  • Dust ejection speed: 20–2 kilometers per second
  • Effective surface temperature: Below 1000 degrees Kelvin ruled out

Timeline

On July 21, 2025, the Hubble Space Telescope captured an image of 3I/ATLAS, revealing a glow indicative of dust evaporation. Subsequent analysis suggested that the glow might be the result of a central light source rather than sunlight reflection. Research continues to determine the characteristics and origins of this interstellar object.

On the evening of the soccer cup match at Harvard’s Institute for Theory & Computation, Avi Loeb, who serves as director, scored two goals for the faculty team, which lost 3-2 to the students. The following morning, he began further analysis of 3I/ATLAS.

Official statements

Avi Loeb stated, "The simplest interpretation is that the nucleus of 3I/ATLAS produces most of the light."
Loeb further noted, "The more data we collect on 3I/ATLAS, the closer we will get to understanding its nature."

Context

3I/ATLAS is one of several interstellar objects observed in recent years, including 1I/`Oumuamua and 2I/Borisov. These previous encounters have sparked interest in the characteristics and origins of such objects, contributing to ongoing research in astrophysics.

What’s next

The HiRISE team on the Mars Reconnaissance Orbiter is expected to observe 3I/ATLAS during its close approach in early October 2025, aiming to gather more data to better understand its properties and behavior.
