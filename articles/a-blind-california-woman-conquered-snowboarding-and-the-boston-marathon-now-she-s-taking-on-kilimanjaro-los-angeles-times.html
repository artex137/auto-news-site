A Blind Bay Area Woman Takes On Kilimanjaro: Is This the Next Great Athletic Feat or a Reckless Stunt?

In a story that sounds like it was plucked straight from the pages of an inspirational novel, Kristie Colton, a 28-year-old blind woman from Mountain View, California, is gearing up to scale the heights of Mt. Kilimanjaro this October. But wait—before we all start applauding this remarkable feat, let’s take a closer look at the facts and the motivations behind this ambitious climb. Is it truly a testament to the human spirit, or are we witnessing a reckless gamble masked as a heroic journey?

Colton, who was diagnosed with Stargardt disease at the tender age of 9, is no stranger to overcoming obstacles. This degenerative eye condition has left her with significant vision loss, but that hasn’t stopped her from achieving some jaw-dropping accomplishments. She learned to snowboard and completed the Boston Marathon, partnered with her friends Jungyeon Park and Grace Eysenbach, who have been instrumental in her athletic endeavors.

“I put a lot of mental energy into trying to hide it, but it did become harder and harder to hide,” Colton said, reflecting on her struggle with vision loss.

But is Colton's climb up Kilimanjaro just another athletic achievement, or is it a calculated risk? With a staggering 66% success rate for the 30,000 people who attempt the hike each year, the mountain is no walk in the park, even for seasoned climbers. Eleven blind climbers, each paired with a sighted guide, will be attempting to reach the nearly 20,000-foot peak alongside Colton. One has to wonder: are these climbers ready for the physical and mental challenges that await them?

Colton’s training regimen has certainly been intense, focusing on heavy cardio and practice hikes. “I’m still working out how to use my hiking poles to sort of feel the ground,” she disclosed, highlighting the complexities of navigating varied terrains. Is this preparation enough for a climb that has seen its fair share of accidents? Just last week, a solo female climber in California had a near-fatal fall, with her injury so severe that “the bone was sticking through her skin.” This raises alarm bells about the dangers that lie ahead for Colton and her team.

“My immediate gut reaction was, ‘No,’” Colton admitted when first approached about the climb. “But then I realized this is going to be a really once-in-a-lifetime opportunity.”

Colton’s story is undeniably inspiring, but it also begs the question: is this a story about personal triumph, or a spectacle for public consumption? The Vorden Initiative, a nonprofit founded by Colton and her friends, aims to educate sighted individuals on how to assist those who are blind. They claim that resources for sighted allies are sorely lacking. “There’s a lot of nonprofits and resources for blind people to do things a certain way, but there really isn’t a resource for sighted people to become allies,” Park stated. This noble cause is commendable, but does it justify the risks involved in such a perilous expedition?

Colton herself reflects on her journey with a sense of wonder. “When I was younger, I really didn’t know what my disease was going to lead to in my life… I didn’t know what my life was going to amount to,” she said. Now, as she prepares for this monumental climb, she enjoys the support of her close friends who have become her eyes on the ground. However, is this friendship enough to mitigate the life-threatening risks they may encounter on Kilimanjaro?

As the group gears up for their trip in late September, one must ponder the potential implications of their journey. Will it merely serve as a form of entertainment for spectators, or will it genuinely raise awareness about the capabilities of individuals with disabilities? The climb is intended not just as a personal challenge, but as a platform to educate others about the spectrum of visual impairments. “It is such a wide spectrum, and how it affects people is very different,” Eysenbach noted.

“I am a 28-year-old woman who lost her eyesight and yet I get to live independently with some of my best friends,” Colton reflected, as she embraces the adventure ahead.

As we follow Kristie Colton’s journey to the summit of Kilimanjaro, one thing is certain: her story is captivating. But as we cheer her on, let's not forget the potential dangers lurking in the shadows. Will this be a groundbreaking moment in the realm of adaptive sports, or a cautionary tale of ambition gone awry? Only time will tell.
