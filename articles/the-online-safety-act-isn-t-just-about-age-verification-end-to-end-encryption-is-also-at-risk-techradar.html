The Online Safety Act isn't just about age verification – end-to-end encryption is also at risk - TechRadar

Published 2025-08-13 16:32 UTC

The Online Safety Act: A Recipe for a Surveillance State?

The internet has been on fire since July 25, 2023, the day the UK’s Online Safety Act came crashing down like a ton of bricks. This legislation, which mandates age verification for accessing adult content, has raised eyebrows among privacy advocates and technologists alike. But hold on! While most are fixated on the age verification fiasco, there’s a much darker cloud looming over our digital rights: the potential dismantling of end-to-end encryption.

That’s right, folks! The Online Safety Act is a sprawling 300-page monstrosity that has critics fuming over its so-called "spy clause." Section 122 of this bill demands tech companies engage in client-side scanning of private messages. That’s right—your private chats could soon be scrutinized for illegal content like terrorism or child sexual abuse material. This draconian requirement, critics argue, is not just an invasion of privacy but a direct assault on the very fabric of our digital security.

“Client-side scanning… cannot happen without breaking the security infrastructure of encrypted services,” warns Robin Wilton, Senior Director for Internet Trust at the Internet Society.

In a world where messaging apps like WhatsApp and Signal have threatened to exit the UK rather than comply with these invasive measures, one has to wonder: what’s next? The government has temporarily halted these scanning provisions until they can figure out a “technically feasible” way to implement them. But let’s be real: this is merely a pause, not a cancellation.

Wilton outlines two ominous paths the authorities might take. The first is to scan messages before they are even encrypted. Imagine the implications of that! Preemptively monitoring everyone’s communications would be an unprecedented violation of privacy rights. In the EU, such a move would trigger outrage, likely being labeled as a “general monitoring obligation.”

The second, even more chilling option, would involve forcing device manufacturers to create a “technical switch” that law enforcement can flip to access users’ chats before they are encrypted. This is not a mere inconvenience; it’s a blueprint for mass surveillance embedded in every device we own.

“That’s putting the technology of mass surveillance onto every device that everyone has,” Wilton cautions.

But wait, there’s more! Encryption isn’t just about keeping your private messages safe; it’s the backbone of our digital communication. Without end-to-end encryption, services like Proton Mail and top-tier VPNs become useless, leaving our data exposed to prying eyes. Yet, law enforcement agencies have long argued that this level of security is a hindrance to their investigations, pushing for the creation of an encryption backdoor. And guess what? The UK government is flirting with this dangerous idea!

Digital rights experts are sounding the alarm, warning that an encryption backdoor could lead to catastrophic consequences. Citizens would be left vulnerable to not only government overreach but also exploitation by cybercriminals. Imagine a scenario where hostile governments gain access to our private data or where cybercriminals hack into a system exploiting this very backdoor. The ramifications would be nothing short of disastrous.

“Imagine being able to attack the digital infrastructure of a country by using a botnet that is exploiting every single Android phone. It would be catastrophic,” warns Wilton.

But wait, the nightmare doesn't end there! The UK is not alone in this surveillance odyssey. The European Union is also pushing for measures dubbed “Chat Control,” aiming to decrypt private messages by 2030. The Online Safety Act is just the tip of the iceberg when it comes to the UK’s descent into a surveillance state.

To add fuel to the fire, an amendment to the Investigatory Powers Act (IPA) now requires tech firms to seek government approval before releasing new security features. This means that companies like Apple and Meta must block or disable security features at the behest of the Home Office without notifying users. Just last year, a Technical Capability Notice under the IPA forced Apple to abandon its iCloud end-to-end encryption feature in the UK. This is not merely a technical issue; it’s a fundamental shift in how we view privacy.

“These companies are being held liable for the behavior of their users and turned into enforcement agencies,” Wilton states.

So, where do we go from here? The enforcement timeline for the Online Safety Act remains murky, leaving us on the edge of our seats. While age verification may seem like the primary concern, it’s merely the beginning of a much larger privacy and security nightmare. As experts have aptly put it, this is a “privacy tradeoff” that no one should be willing to accept.

The Online Safety Act is not just a regulatory framework; it's a potential gateway to a dystopian future where our every digital move is monitored. Are we really ready to hand over our privacy in the name of safety? The stakes couldn’t be higher! Buckle up, folks; we are in for a bumpy ride.
